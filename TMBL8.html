Centrality measures

Centrality measures provide a way to quantify a nodes 'importance'

  • Which brain regions should a new drug target? Which regions surgically removed to prevent epileptic seizures?

  • Which genes are under/over expressed in cancerous tissues? Etc.

  • Identifying key reactions (metabolites) controlling local information flow in directed reaction-centric graphs of microbial metabolic networks can contribute to our understanding of how metabolic pathways are assembled.

We shall see a number of examples of such measures

Degree centrality

The simplest measure of a node's importance is given by the network degree

Her a node $i$ is considered more important than node $j$ if

\[ k_i > k_j \] where \[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ \begin{align*} k_i &= \sum_{j}a_{ij}\\ &=\left(A\mathbb{1}\right)_i \end{align*} }}} \]

Degree centrality naturally extends to directed networks via the in- and out-degrees

Eigenvector centrality

A natural extension of the degree centrality is given by the so-called eigenvector centrality

Here we quantify a nodes importance by considering its neighbours importance i.e. a node is important if it is connected to lots of important nodes

Mathematically we say

\[ \begin{align*} e_i &= \frac{1}{\lambda}\sum_{j\in M(i)}e_j\\ &=\frac{1}{\lambda}\sum_{j}a_{ij}e_j \end{align*} \]

$M(i)$ is the set of neighbours of node $i$

The score $e_i$ of node $i$ is proportional to its neighbours scores

We can rewrite the equation for eigenvector centrality using matrix-vector notation as

\[ \begin{align*} \begin{pmatrix} e_1\\e_2\\\vdots\\e_n \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\lambda} \begin{pmatrix} a_{11}&\ldots&a_{1n}\\ \vdots&&\vdots\\ a_{n1}&\ldots&a_{nn} \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} e_1\\e_2\\\vdots\\e_n \end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \]

Or

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ A\mathbf{e} = \lambda_1 \mathbf{e} }}} \]

Since we require $\mathbf{e}$ to have non-negative entries we choose the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector

Katz centrality

  • We can also rank nodes according to their ability to 'communicate' with the rest of the network

  • Here communication is measured in terms of walks, i.e. nodes that are connected by lots of walks are considered more important

  • Recalling that $A^k$ counts the number of walks of length $k$ in the network motivates

    • \[ \left(\alpha^0A^0 + \alpha^1A^1+\cdots+\alpha^kA^k+\cdots\right)\mathbb{1} \]

  • Note that

    \[ \left(A^k\mathbb{1}\right)_i \]

    counts the number of walks of length $k$ from node $i$ to all other nodes

The series in brackets i.e.

\[ \alpha^0A^0 + \alpha^1A^1+\cdots+\alpha^kA^k+\cdots \]

converges if $\alpha\in (0, 1/\rho(A))$, where $\rho(A)$ denotes the spectral radius

Thus for $\alpha\in (0, 1/\rho(A))$ we can write

\[ \sum_{k=0}^\infty \alpha^kA^k = \left(I-\alpha A\right)^{-1} \]

Which results in the so-called Katz centrality measure:

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ \begin{align*} K_i&=\left[\left(\sum_{k=0}^\infty \alpha^kA^k\right)\mathbb{1} \right]_i\\ &=\left[\left(I-\alpha A\right)^{-1}\mathbb{1}\right]_i \end{align*} }}} \]

Betweenness centrality

  • The final centrality measure we shall consider is the so-called betweenness centrality

  • This measure differs in that the most 'important' node is not necessarily the one that is most 'connected' at least in the traditional sense

  • This idea is illustrated in the above figure - from a BC point of view node $A$ is the most important, as we shall seeâ€¦

Betweenness centrality is defined as follows

\[ \color{red}{\boxed{\color{white}{ B_i = \sum_{s\ne t\neq i}\frac{n^i_{st}}{g_{st}} }}} \]

Here

\[ \begin{align*} n^i_{st} &- \quad \# \text{ shortest paths between $s, t$ passing through $i$}\\ g_{st} &- \quad \# \text{ shortest paths between $s, t$} \end{align*} \]

and $B_i$ denotes the score of node $i$
